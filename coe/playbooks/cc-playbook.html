Contact Center Playbook

Introduction

The GSA Center of Excellence (CoE) for Contact Center of the Future developed a playbook consisting of 15 plays that Government Agencies should consider when looking to modernize, optimize and consolidate contact centers. The President’s Management Agenda (PMA) and Cross-Agency Priority (CAP) Goals showcase the Government’s focus on improving the customer experience with accessing information and services. Contact centers play a crucial role in an agency’s CAP goal strategy, since they are a critical touch point to understanding the customer experience and identifying opportunities that can be fixed or improved.

The plays are organized as a step-by-step task list, beginning with understanding customer experience and ending with ensuring changes last. The playbook provides an overview of key questions and topics to consider for implementing each play. This high-level view will enable agencies across government to operationalize learnings drawn from CoE discovery efforts at USDA.

Play 1

Understand the Current Customer Experience and “Real” Nature of the Work

As you begin to understand the experience your customers (e.g., farmers, ranchers, producers, foresters, etc.) are having with the people and systems delivered by your agency, you can identify areas that will need attention and further analysis. It’s also important to determine the appetite for change within your agency (e.g., incremental change vs. transformational change). If the agency is a citizen-facing agency, more than likely you deliver services and information to citizens online or via the phone, email, or chat through contact centers.

This transitions you from managing people to managing the system--from end-to-end. You go from “making the numbers” to “serving the customers.” It’s important to remove the causes of the failure work demand which will improve customer service and reduce costs.

Checklist

  1. Understand who is responsible for customer experience within the agency
  2. Understand who is responsible for websites and contact centers
  3. Understand whether a centralized knowledge base feeds the websites and contact centers
  4. Understand who are your customers
  5. What percentage of the calls are value demand and what percentage are failure demand?
  6. What are the primary reasons why people call, email, or chat the contact center?
  7. Are there any issues that are repetitive?

Key Questions

  • Are your customers satisfied with the level of service they receive? Is your agency positioned to address improvements?
  • How can you identify the root causes and remedies for the failure based interactions and deliver outstanding service to the value based interactions?
Play 2

Understand Your Customers

Truly understanding your customers and what matters to them is at the core of providing outstanding experiences. If you don’t know what the customers’ expectations are, you don’t know if you’ve met or exceeded those expectations. In the private sector, companies that know how and when to use the wide array of research tools available have big competitive advantage in generating insights that lead to growth. In the government, understanding your customers requires you to understand what motivates them, what matters to them and and how they make decisions. This allows you to serve them without wasting valuable resources on things that don’t matter to them.

Checklist

  1. Who are your customers?
  2. What are the primary personas represented by your customers?
  3. How do you currently collect customer feedback?
  4. Can you observe customers while they are making decisions?
  5. What surveys do you currently use? What do you collect?
  6. What tools do you have within your agency to conduct research to better your customers?

Key Questions

  • Do you really understand your customers from their current satisfaction to insights on the ways they would like services delivered in the future?
Play 3

Understand Your Workforce

Contact centers are staffed by either government employees or contracted out. There can also be technology within contact centers that help to answer and route calls. When trying to manage contact centers across an enterprise, by understanding the complexity and topic of the calls and the staff answering the calls and managing the operations gives you insights into future strategies. For example, if most of the calls are complex in nature pertaining to the mission of the agency, it may be difficult to contract out those calls. On the other hand, some agencies outsource contact centers that handle fairly complex technical and medical issues. If a large percentage of the calls are asking routine and low complexity questions, the answers to those calls can be automated. The critical component is a knowledge base that clearly answers the questions to the most frequently asked questions in plain language.

Training is an important component to managing the workforce in a contact center. In some cases, an independent quality assurance program combined with training is used to maintain the level of proficiency and professionalism needed to perform at peak.

Checklist

  1. Understand the number of customer service representatives (operations and managers) in each contact center
  2. Understand the skill levels needed within the contact center
  3. Understand the grade structure within the contact center
  4. Understand how the workforce is managed if the contact center operates over a 24 hour period
  5. How is a workforce management tool used to schedule the staff?
  6. Are there any issues with the workforce in terms of engagement or skills?
  7. How do you train your workforce for maximum proficiency and customer experience
  8. Are “at home” agents used?
  9. Do you use requirements for companies to use “at home” or “vets” as customer service reps?

Key Questions

  • What are the best ways to optimize the staff in a contact center to provide the best service?
Play 4

Understand Your Employees

Because customer experience is based on the interactions that your customers have with every program, initiative, service and employee, having employees that are “ready and willing” to deliver service is a must. This is a critical component to changing the culture in an agency and becoming truly customer-focused. Leading customer-focused companies have employees that are engaged and motivated to deliver outstanding customer experience. They understand the vision that the company’s products and services are supposed to have on their customers’ lives. They are also empowered by the leadership to do the right thing in bringing good service to the customers.

Checklist

  1. Are there any metrics in your agency that tells the story about whether your workforce is engaged and motivated?
  2. What policies and procedures are in place to empower the workforce to deliver services?
  3. Do performance plans reflect customer-centric goals and objectives?
  4. How are employees for customer-focused achievements and successes?
  5. Are there customer experience champions located throughout the organization?

Key Questions

  • Do you have a customer-forced workforce?
Play 5

Understand the Entire Customer Experience from Beginning to End

You need to understand the different ways people will interact with your services, including the actions they take online, through a mobile application, on a phone, chat or email, or in person. By understanding the full journey, you can understand how the customer interacts with the services; how long each step in the process takes; identify moments of truth, pain points or opportunities to improve both the experience when interacting with the contact center and the overall customer experience.

Checklist

  1. Understand the different points at which people will interact with the service – both online and in person
  2. Identify pain points in the current way users interact with the service, and prioritize these according to user needs
  3. Ensure that the contact center touchpoints are integrated into the online touch points people use to interact with the service

Key Questions

  • Does your agency understand the experience of their customers in all channels from the time a need arises through a transaction (including pain points, moments of truth)?
Play 6

Conduct and Inventory of Current Contact Centers

To fully understand the current environment and manage the services at the enterprise level, you need to understand the current “as is” environment within your agency. It’s critical to understand the number of contact centers, the technology that drives each of them from infrastructure to interactive voice response (IVR) to knowledge base etc, whether they are staffed by government or contractor staff, the call volume and type of calls, and what the current and customer experience metrics are.

Checklist

  1. Is there anyone or an organization that is currently responsible for contact centers across the agency?
  2. How do you determine who to send a data call to?
  3. What is the best approach to collecting the inventory?
  4. What should be collected in a contact center inventory to make decisions?
    1. How many contact centers are in the agency?
    2. How many 1-800 numbers do you have in your agency?
    3. Which contact centers and/or 1-800 numbers deliver what programs?
    4. Are they staffed by government or contractors? How many customer service representatives and managers are in each contact center?
    5. What channels do each contact center handle (e.g., calls, emails, web chats, emails, social media, etc)
    6. How many calls, emails, chats do each contact center handle?
    7. What is the complexity of the calls - simple (tier 1), complex (tier 2), or transactional (tier 3)?
    8. How do the contact centers measure success? Operational metrics? Key performance indicators?
    9. Who is the primary point of contact for each contact center?
    10. What program area does your contact center fall in?
    11. What technology is used?
      1. IVR
      2. Telephony
      3. Customer Resource Management (CRM)
      4. Knowledge Database
      5. Cloud

Key Questions

  • Do you know what you currently have?
Play 7

Determine the Maturity of Each Contact Center

Each contact center should be assessed independently to determine the maturity in terms of its people, processes and technology as well as the success at delivering services to its customers. The following areas should be assessed:

  • Agency Desktop and CRM
  • Telephony Platform and IVR
  • Hosting, Network, and Security
  • Workforce Management
  • Knowledge/Content Management
  • Quality Management
  • Training
  • Operations Delivery Model and Operating Standard Operating Procedures Library
  • Reporting

Checklist

  1. Have you completed an inventory of the contact centers?
  2. Leverage the best practices from the most mature contact centers with the less mature contact centers
  3. Are there actions that can be taken to raise the level of maturity across all of the contact centers?
  4. Are there policies and procedures or service levels that need to be created to increase maturity?

Key Questions

  • Where are there strategic improvements that need to be made to improve operational efficiency and customer experience?
Play 8

Identify and Select Best-in-Class Contact Center Best Practices

Best-in-class (BIC) contact centers are those contact centers that implement best-in-class people, processes and technologies to deliver outstanding services. A BIC contact center continually analyzes both the key performance indicators and the customer satisfaction to determine where improvements need to made. In addition, a BIC contact center continually innovates to improve performance and ensures the privacy and security of the customers.

BIC contact centers contain the following capabilities:

    Omni-channel Experience

  • Digital communications strategy
  • “Shift left” philosophy--shifting to lower tiers for cost savings
  • CRM and other tech platforms (e.g., data analytics, interaction history, document upload, etc.)

    • Innovation

  • Ongoing innovation for efficiencies and experience--demonstrate cost reduction year over year and quality improvement

    • Security and privacy

  • Do you need to protect Personally Identifiable Information (PII)?

    • Quality Assurance

  • Monitoring resolution actions to close the loops
  • Listen to recorded or live call and evaluate performance of agents
  • Case management system ensures tracking of customer inquiry resolution (end-to-end view of resolution)

    • Performance Management

  • Regular reporting of overall and individual performance
  • Resolve at first contact; first call resolution (usually about 88%)
  • Proper contact resolution
  • Ongoing feedback collection and innovative strategies

    • Staffing Optimization

  • Continuity of operations
  • Surge/overflow strategy
  • Peak/off-peak: flexible work strategy
  • Virtual agent strategy and impact on recruitment

    • Change Management

  • Cultural change within the agency (bring in someone who operates in change management, conditioning people to train them)
  • Employee engagement

    • Acquisition Support

  • Acquisition best practices
  • Transition clauses
  • Hybrid by tiers
  • Labor categories
  • Specify skillsets
  • Integration with other business functions/apps

    • Training and Development

  • Training (agent behavior, soft skills)
  • Recruitment (retention, turnover)
  • Screening for language skills and clearance

Checklist

  1. What are the types of services being delivered through the contact center?
  2. How do you compare to a BIC contact center?
  3. What are the roadblocks/challenges to becoming a BIC contact center?

Key Questions

  • What are the steps to transform to a best-in-class contact center?
Play 9

Create a Roadmap: Design the Contact Center Experience of the Future

Designing the customers’ future experience begin with designing it from the customer’s perspective. It should include all of the touchpoints in the customer journey. It’s important to integrate all of the delivery channels and to leverage the data from each channel to improve the overall journey.

A roadmap will identify the steps it takes to achieve the desired vision in a phased approach. It may provide the possible options or alternatives to achieve the desired state with the benefits and risks of each with recommendations for the most cost effective solution.

Checklist

  1. Ensure that you understand the current contact center environment, customer experience, and customer journey
  2. Determine the guiding principles for the future state experience
  3. What are the alternatives, benefits and risks of each
  4. Do you have one central number for the agency’s customers to call?
  5. Do you want to have a directory of services for the agents to be able to identify the major points of contact for various services across the agency?
  6. Alternative Solutions (low-, mid- and high-level solutions)
  7. Governance structure—how will the contact center be managed, and by whom?
  8. Technology (what is available now that can be leveraged, what should be procured?)
  9. Multi-channel vs. omni-channel
  10. Leveraging the use of artificial intelligence (AI)
  11. Authentication
  12. Staffing/leveraging the use of existing resources (insource or outsource or mixed)
  13. High-level steps in the roadmap with measurable outcomes

Key Questions

  • What do you want your Contact Center to be when it grows up?
  • How do you transform digital services by transforming contact centers?
Play 10

Ensure that Contact Center Services are Included in a Customer Strategy

While very few federal agencies have customer strategies, it is a best practice to provide a focus and priority on improving services. A customer strategy ensures that all channels are integrated, that the customers are defined, customer expectations are experiences are documented, priority initiatives are identified and targets are set.

Intention: Agencies don’t have customer strategies

  • Create customer strategy that includes all channels (including contact centers)

Checklist

  1. Who is responsible for creating the customer strategy?
  2. Is customer experience included in agency’s strategic plan?
  3. Does the IT modernization plan include contact centers and customer experience as a goal for the plan?
  4. Who is responsible for the contact centers?
  5. What’s the governance structure for customer experience and the contact centers?
  6. Who are your customers and what is their expectations?
  7. Do you know what matters to customers?
  8. How will you measure performance?
  9. Understand the integration between the contact center and websites

Key Questions

  • Is your contact center, customer experience, and digital strategy aligned?
Play 11

Measure the Contact Center’s Service: Continually Monitor and Improve

The purpose of monitoring measures is twofold: first, to assess the customer’s satisfaction with the service provided by the contact center and two, to ensure that it operates as efficiently and effectively as possible. In addition to customer satisfaction measures (outside in) and key performance indicators (inside out), it’s important to measure the type of calls being received. These measures are important to driving an outstanding experience and containing costs, and should be continually monitored to determine the operational efficiency of the contact centers. As the performance is monitored and measured, you should assess what actions need to be taken to improve a specified performance issue or customer pain point.

Checklist

  1. Develop metrics that will measure how well the service is meeting user needs at each step of the service
  2. What specific interactions do you want to measure?
  3. Operational Measures:
  4. First Call Resolution
  5. Average Speed to Answer
  6. Average Handle Time
  7. Abandonment Rate
  8. Percent of calls that completed the transaction
  9. Customer Satisfaction Measures
  10. Are there any existing feedback mechanisms that could be leveraged?
  11. What metrics are reported to your agency? To the Office of Management and Budget (OMB)?
  12. Is there a process in place for regular check-ins to diagnose opportunities to review data and improve service?
  13. Understand whether there is a process in place to leverage data for service improvements across channels

Key Questions

  • How do I know if we are succeeding?
  • How do I stay on top?
Play 12

Conduct Test and Learn Initiatives: Infuse Innovation

Innovation can mean introducing new types of technology that disrupts the current way that the contact center operates. Technology can make agents more efficient, provide analytics that can result in improved processes. Innovation can be introduced by an agency in the processes they use to manage the contact center and leverage the data across channels. Innovation can also be introduced in the contracting process.

Continuous improvement can only be successful by experimenting on technologies to implement to improve processes. Test and learn initiatives are small scale implementations or experiments using new technology to evaluate the feasibility, time, cost, and challenges to determine whether it’s possible to move to a full-scale project.It’s best to have a clear idea about how you want to create and evaluate the pilot.

Checklist

  1. Understand what actions have been taken to bring innovative technologies into the contact center
  2. Include innovations in technologies, improved business processes or economies of scale
  3. Determine the best mechanism for incentivizing innovation (e.g., Value Engineering Improvements)
  4. How many test and learn initiatives does the agency want to assess?
  5. Understand any agency cultural issues that could restrict your ability to conduct test and learn
  6. What areas could benefit from testing new technology?
  7. Where are the pain points in the customer journey that could benefit from innovative thinking?

Key Questions

  • How do we ensure that companies and agencies are incentivized to innovate and improve the experience across all channels?
  • How can we test new technologies that may be able to improve the customer’s experience and reduce cost?
Play 13

Incorporate Contracting Best Practices for Best-in-Class Digital Contact Center Services

Contracting for contact centers can present a myriad of complexities because many agencies want to acquire a company that can provide the entire contact center products and services (i.e., contact center agents, facilities, technology infrastructure, telecom infrastructure, analytics). When contracting for contact centers, there are many things to consider:

  1. What contract would you use to be able to acquire the products and services you need?
  2. The type of contract (e.g., firm fixed price or cost plus) would be the most advantageous. Different contract types can be used for different parts of the contract.
  3. The type of requirements to be released (e.g., statement of objectives, performance based contract, performance work statement).

Checklist

  1. What are the type of products and services you need (e.g., full contact center, infrastructure, customer service representatives, test and learn initiatives, training, quality assurance, etc.)?
  2. What experience are you driving for each component of the contact center?
  3. Are there some tasks where you can define the requirement clearly and award on a fixed price basis?
  4. How do you leverage the contracting process to incentivize contractors to innovation throughout the contract life?

Key Questions

  • How can we contract for best-in-class contact center services?
Play 14

Create and Leverage a Knowledge Management System

Knowledge is one of the critical success factors to providing outstanding service to customers. Knowledge management articles are a way to document the frequently asked questions. Benefits to the government include:

  1. Provides flexibility in future customer strategies such as the implementation of artificial intelligence
  2. Promotes efficiency, since it reduces the time it takes for agents to find and deliver content

When determining how your contact center representatives are going to provide answers to solve the numerous types of questions to solve your customers’ problems, you need to understand what knowledge is and how to create a system to manage. To begin, start with the frequently asked questions that are online across all the contact centers and digital properties. Regardless of the current technology being used, migrating knowledge from one technology to another requires reconfiguring and editing the content. The time it takes to create a knowledge management system should not be underestimated.

Checklist

  1. What kind of knowledge management systems are used in the agency?
  2. What kind of content management system is used to deliver content to the website?
  3. What kind of knowledge is needed to solve the customers’ most important problems?
  4. How long will it take to create a viable knowledge management system?
  5. How can technology be used to accelerate the creation of a knowledge management database?

Key Questions

  • What is the best strategy for using a knowledge management system that allows you to drive a best-in-class contact center?
Play 15

Ensure that the Changes Last

When modernizing, optimizing or consolidating contact centers, you will encounter many places where everyone wants to maintain the “status quo.” People are always sensitive to how any changes will impact their job, roles, and responsibilities. Leadership should assess the culture of the organization to determine the changes to make their citizen services strategies a success. Any changes to a contact center strategy will probably impact their online and in-person strategy. Some of the issues that could present change management issues include:

  1. Implementing new technology which results in learning new ways of operating.
  2. Using contractor staff or government employees.
  3. Changing the customer journey in terms of new phone numbers to call; there will be changes to digital properties as well as anything that touches the customers.

All of these changes require buy-in from all levels within the agency and the need to justify the changes in terms of customer experience and the return on investment (i.e., efficiency and effectiveness).

Checklist

  1. Does the leadership support and make a customer-focused strategy a priority within the agency?
  2. Does the leadership support your contact center strategy?
  3. Do the employees within the agency understand the importance of customer-centered culture?
  4. What’s the best way to inform the agency about the changes to answering customer questions?
  5. How long will it take to make increase awareness in the strategy?
  6. How do you leverage the supporters of the strategy?
  7. How do you identify the possible detractors?
  8. What is the return on investment (RoI) for your strategy?

Key Questions

  • What are the factors that would ensure that your contact center strategy could be implemented and sustained?
